Moon at Perigee and Apogee: 
 2001 to  2100




Greenwich Mean Time








Introduction




The Moon revolves around Earth in an elliptical orbit with a mean eccentricity of 0.0549. 
As a result, the Moon's distance from Earth (center-to-center) varies with mean values of 363,396 km at perigee (closest) to 405,504 km at apogee (most distant). 




The Moon's actual distances at apogee and perigee can vary by a few percent due to the gravitaional perturbations of the Sun on the Moon's eccentric orbit.
Over the 5000-year period from -1999 to 3000 (2000 BCE to 3000 CE), 
the distance of the Moon's perigee varies from 356,355 to 370,399 km 
while the Moon's apogee varies from 404,042 to 406,725 km 
(Five Millennium Catalog of Solar Eclipses, Espenak and Meeus, 2021). 




The anomalistic month is defined as the time it takes the Moon to make one revolution around its orbit with respect to the perigee.
The length of the mean anomalistic month as calculated for the year 2000 is 27.55455 days (27d 13h 18m 33s). 
However, the actual duration can vary by several days due to gravitaional perturbations of the Sun and Earth on the Moon's eccentric orbit.




Over the 5000-year period from -1999 to 3000, the shortest anomalistic month is 24.629 days (2.925 days shorter than the mean) while the longest anomalistic month is 28.565 days (1.011 days longer than the mean). 




The table presented here gives the date and time (Greenwich Mean Time) of all perigees and apogees of the Moon for the century 
 2001 to  2100
 (for the years 1501 through 2500, see  Perigee and Apogee by Century). 




The Moon's geocentric distance and apparent diameter (arc-minute) are given for both perigee and apogee. 
The 'm' and 'M' after some distances indicate the minimum and maximum values, respectively, each year. 
For conversion from Greenwich Mean Time to other time zones, see Time Zones and Universal Time.
